Studying the sensitivity of Fermi LAT and, in particular, applying the LAT's Low-energy (LLE) technique is one of the main goals of our work in Crnogorčević et al. 2021 (publicly available on arXiv). Here, we conduct a data-driven sensitivity analysis to find the distance limit for a hypothetical ALP detection with the LLE technique which, in contrast to the standard LAT analysis, allows for a a larger effective area for energies down to 30 MeV. We also select a candidate sample of twenty-four GRBs and carry out a model comparison analysis in which we consider different GRB spectral models with and without an ALP signal component. In this paper, you can learn about the statistical methods used in our analysis (Wilks' theorem to the rescue!) and the underlying physical assumptions, the feasibility of the upper limits on ALP coupling from our model comparison results, and an outlook on future MeV instruments in the context of ALP searches.

To the left, we have a plot that shows distance limits for a hypothetical ALP detection using the LLE technique, for a 10-solar-mass progenitor. The green, black, and solid red lines represent the 5-sigma detection limits on distances with so-far observed background levels from our GRB sample, while the yellow solid line represents the expected 90% confidence level upper limit for the median background. The dotted vertical line in the plot shows the upper limit for the ALP-photon coupling derived from literature. Finally, the gray hatching shows the parameter space to which LLE is sensitive. So, for example, for the low background (green line) the upper distance limit is ~4.4 Mpc; for the median background it is ~4.9 Mpc, and for the highest background we can reach ~6.6 Mpc.

In my opinion, this is an interesting result---and somewhat counterintuitive! It seems that if we have higher background levels (i.e. more noise in our data), we can detect ALPs that originate from sources further away, and when we have less background (i.e. "cleaner" data) we do not seem to reach quite as far. Why do you think that's the case?

(Work in progress) The ALP emission precedes that of the jet outbreak after a collapse of a massive star. With the observationally suggested causal connection between CCSNe and long gamma-ray bursts (lGRBs), a spectral analysis of the Fermi-observed lGRBs with precursor emission provides an unexplored venue in the context of ALP searches. A comprehensive analysis of lGRB precursors to inform whether their origin can be explained with ALPs is currently underway, with the expected paper submission in early 2023.


To the left, we have an example of precursor emission in the GRB 120624B LLE 0.5-sec binned lightcurve (in purple), with overlaid Bayesian blocks (black outline). Within the red, dashed lines is where 90% of the total lGRB fluence is emitted. Note the precursor emission at ~270 seconds prior to the main emission!

Several theoretical models have been proposed to address the astrophysical origin of the precursor emission; however, currently no single model can explain its astrophysical origin. Here, we hypothesize that this precursor phenomenon could be accounted for by the ALP-induced burst that is released before the main gamma-ray jet emission. Stay tuned!

The precursor analysis in lGRBs is funded through the Fermi GI Cycle 15 program (PI: Crnogorčević).



I'm currently a Postdoctoral Fellow at the Oskar Klein Centre at Stockholm University working within Prof. Timothy Linden’s astroparticle group. I am interested in studying various high-energy phenomena, primarily focusing on the searches for the new physics using multimessenger astronomy. Currently, I work on studying observational signatures of axions and axion-like particles from transient astrophysical sources. I am also active within the ongoing multimessenger efforts and work on studying the origins of astrophysical neutrinos; as well as conduct offline, follow-up searches for electromagnetic counterparts to gravitational wave observations from compact binary mergers.

Prior to coming to Stockholm, I obtained my doctorate degree from the University of Maryland/NASA Goddard.

My homes are scattered across three continents and I am always planning a flight home.

Announcement [April 27, 2023]: I defended my dissertation on April 24, 2023, "New Messengers & New Physics: A Survey of the High-energy Universe."
